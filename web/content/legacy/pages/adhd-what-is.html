Plain-language overview (for parents & carers)

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder) describes a pattern of difficulties with attention, organisation, activity level and impulse control that starts in childhood and can continue into adult life.   • Attention: it can be hard to start tasks, stay focused or finish without getting side-tracked.
  • Organisation: everyday things like packing a bag, remembering homework or paying bills can feel overwhelming.
  • Activity level: some people are very â€œon the goâ€; others feel mainly mentally restless.
  • Impulse control: acting or speaking quickly, interrupting or making snap decisions.

Patterns, types and life stages

ADHD looks different from person to person and changes with age. Professionals often describe three presentations:

  • Predominantly inattentive: daydreamy, forgetful, loses things, struggles to finish tasks.
  • Predominantly hyperactive-impulsive: very active, restless, talks a lot, acts quickly.
  • Combined: a mix of both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive features.

In younger children, ADHD may show as climbing, running, loud play and big emotions. In teenagers and adults, restlessness can become more internal â€“ feeling driven, overwhelmed or â€œon edgeâ€ even when sitting still.

Clinician snapshot: What ADHD is, in practice

For clinicians and other professionals, ADHD is a heterogeneous neurodevelopmental disorder characterised by developmentally inappropriate levels of inattention, hyperactivity and/or impulsivity, with onset in childhood, persistence across settings and clinically significant impairment.

  • Use a multi-informant, multi-setting assessment: parent/caregiver history, teacher reports, and where appropriate, the young personâ€™s own account.
  • Map symptoms to diagnostic criteria (e.g. DSM-5 / ICD-11) and functional impact at home, school/work and socially.
  • Screen systematically for co-occurring conditions (anxiety, depression, learning disorders, autism, tics, trauma, sleep issues, substance use).
  • Consider differential diagnoses and contextual factors â€“ for example, sensory differences, language needs, bereavement, bullying or chronic stress.
  • Frame ADHD as a long-term condition requiring ongoing review, not a one-off label or prescription.

Detailed guidance on assessment, shared decision-making, medication and psychosocial interventions sits on the later pages (ADHD checks & assessment, Diagnosis & treatment options) and in your local/national guidelines.
