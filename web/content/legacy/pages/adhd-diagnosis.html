Many parents feel pulled in two directions. On one side, a diagnosis can bring understanding and unlock support. On the other, they may worry about stigma, future records, or their child being treated differently.

Possible benefits of a diagnosis

  • A clear explanation for long-standing struggles with focus, organisation or behaviour.
  • Access to structured support at school or college (SEND support, exam access arrangements).
  • Better understanding from family members, friends and teachers.
  • Ability to explore evidence-based treatments, including non-medication options and medication.
  • Validation that challenges are linked to a recognised condition, not â€œbad parentingâ€.

  • â€œWill teachers or employers lower their expectations?â€

  • â€œWhat will family or our community think?â€

After a diagnosis Â· What might happen next?

A diagnosis opens up options; it does not lock anyone into a single route. Plans should be personalised, evidence-informed and reviewed regularly.

Understanding & education

  • Psychoeducation for the young person and family about ADHD and how it affects everyday life.
  • Strengths-based conversation: what the person enjoys and where ADHD may be an advantage.
  • Information for school/college/work to support reasonable adjustments.

Environment & routines

  • Structured routines at home and school, with visual supports and clear expectations.
  • Movement breaks, sensory strategies and sleep hygiene to support regulation.
  • Tools such as timers, planners and digital reminders to reduce â€œworking memory overloadâ€.

Therapies & medication

  • Behavioural and organisational support; parenting programmes for younger children.
  • Talking therapies (for example CBT or coaching) for older children, teens and adults.
  • Medication may be offered where symptoms remain significant, following guidelines and careful monitoring.

You can always ask: â€œWhat are our non-medication options?â€ and â€œHow will we review medication if we try it?â€

Clinician snapshot Â· Shared decision-making in ADHD

This section is written for professionals and assumes familiarity with diagnostic criteria. It focuses on collaborative practice and explaining risk/benefit in accessible terms.

Framing the diagnosis

  • Describe ADHD as a neurodevelopmental pattern with both vulnerabilities and strengths.
  • Make explicit that diagnosis is a tool for planning support, not a judgement on character or parenting.
  • Be transparent about uncertainty, borderline presentations and the option of review over time.
  • Discuss the role of co-occurring conditions and systemic factors (e.g. poverty, exclusion, trauma).

Discussing options & consent

  • Outline evidence-based interventions by age group, including when medication is and is not indicated.
  • Use plain language to explain potential benefits, common side-effects and monitoring plans.
  • Involve children and young people in decisions at a level appropriate to their age and understanding.
  • Document shared decision-making, including family preferences and agreed review points.

Local protocols, national guidelines and individual context should all inform the final plan.
