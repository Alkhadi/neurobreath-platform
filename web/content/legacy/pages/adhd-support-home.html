Routines that work with ADHD, not against it

ADHD brains often struggle with time, sequencing and switching tasks. Predictable routines reduce the number of decisions in a day and make things feel safer for everyone.

Keep it short, visible and repeatable

  • Break mornings and evenings into 3‚Äì6 clear steps, not long "To Do" lists.
  • Use pictures, icons or simple words at eye level rather than long verbal reminders.
  • Pair routines with anchors: wake-up song, same breakfast table set-up, same place for school bag.
  • Practice the routine on calmer days so it feels familiar when you are rushed or stressed.

Praise, rewards and boundaries

Children and teens with ADHD often hear more corrections than praise. Over time, this can chip away at self-esteem. Small, specific praise helps rebuild that balance.

"Catch them doing it almost right"

  • Describe exactly what you like: "I like how you came back to finish your homework."
  • Focus on effort and strategies, not just results: "You used your timer without me asking."
  • Offer small rewards linked to routines: choosing music, picking a game, extra story time.
  • Keep consequences clear and consistent, but avoid long lectures once everyone is upset.

Rewards do not have to be expensive. Attention, time together and choices often matter more than money.

Reward chart ideas

  • Star chart for one or two focus behaviours (e.g. "hang up coat" or "ask for help before shouting").
  • Token jar where small tokens build towards a shared activity (baking, park, movie night).
  • Weekly "spotlights" where you name three things your child did that made you proud.

Transitions, meltdowns and "big feelings"

Many ADHD brains find switching tasks harder than doing the task itself. This can show up as arguments, "digging heels in", or complete shutdown when it is time to leave, stop screens or start homework.

Before the storm

  • Give gentle warnings: "5 more minutes of Xbox, then pause for dinner." Follow with a 2-minute warning.
  • Use visual timers or a sand timer so the change is predictable.
  • Offer choices within limits: "Do you want to walk or scooter to the car?"
  • Keep your own voice calm and low; model the regulation you want them to borrow.

During and after

  • Keep everyone safe first; remove siblings from conflict if needed.
  • Use few words: "You're angry. I am here. We'll sort this when you're calm."
  • Once calm, repair and problem-solve together rather than going over every detail.
  • Notice triggers and patterns so you can adjust routines next time.
